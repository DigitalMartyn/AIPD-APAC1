## 1. The stance

### First, a provocation

Customers hire the team for two things at once:

- **What we make** — the tangible product or output.
- **How we think** — the reasoning, framing, and judgement behind it.

Running a workshop well is where *how we think* becomes visible and valuable to the customer.

### Run the room with curiosity

- **The approach:** Use every design-thinking tool to create clarity and alignment around
  the *technology opportunity*, the *user reality*, and the *business objective*.
- **The outcome:** Shape a product proposition that is **adoptable, scalable, and connected
  to the wider business vision**.

---

## 2. Before the room

### Pre-work

Workshops are for **working**, not for presenting.

- Move presentations and context-setting into pre-work. If someone needs to present, do it
  before the session, not inside it.
- Every minute of workshop time should be spent on the work only the room can do together.
- **Create a cheat sheet:** a one-pager per participant — crib notes, not a script — including
  the agenda and what each person needs to bring.
- **Don't** use the workshop for twenty minutes of slides; that spends the one resource
  (everyone in a room together) on the work that matters least.

### Virtual vs in person

Both work — they just fail differently.

- **In person** is *many-to-many*: presence and energy, artefacts on the wall, side
  conversations that surface signal.
- **Virtual** is *one-to-many*: half the cameras are off, but Copilot tooling and
  transcription help, and it can be easier to include remote operators.

### Copilot tools & transcription

**Always transcribe.** Rules:

1. Don't rely on the transcript summary alone.
2. Challenge the AI — interrogate what it captured.
3. If you're generating outputs live, verify them against what was actually said.
4. Transcription is an aid, not the record of truth.

- **Say this:** *"Was this explicit…"* — check whether a conclusion was actually stated or
  inferred by the tool.
- **Don't** trust the summary blindly: AI summaries flatten nuance, direct quotes disappear,
  and you end up presenting the model's interpretation rather than the room's.

## 3. The mindset — five moves for running the room

| Move | Principle |
|------|-----------|
| **M01** | Frame around purpose, not process |
| **M02** | Work top-down and bottom-up |
| **M03** | Facilitate through provocation & synthesis |
| **M04** | Manage energy, safety & tone |
| **M05** | Come back to value & action |

### M01 · Frame the room around purpose, not process

The workshop is not about the method; it is about *why we're here and what we're trying to
decide*. Frame every activity by its purpose so energy flows to the problem, not the mechanics.

The arc runs **widen → narrow → land**:

1. **Immersion** — listen and understand.
   - Interview users and immerse in their reality.
   - Run tours and walkthroughs.
   - Surface the real operational problem.
2. **Consolidation** — shape and set direction.
   - Shape and cluster what was heard.
   - Run feedback loops.
   - Set direction for what comes next → **the outcome**.

- **Say this:** *"why we're here, what we're deciding…"* (open by naming purpose, not agenda).

### M02 · Work top-down and bottom-up, at the same time

The defining move: hold **strategic altitude** and **operational reality** together. Never let
the room stay abstract for long.

| Strategic altitude — *shape the future* | Operational reality — *stay in the weeds* |
|------------------------------------------|--------------------------------------------|
| Senior stakeholders feel the vision forming | What happens day to day and where comms break |
| AI, digital twins, operating models | How people actually work |
| Transformation horizons and platform strategy | What decisions get made, and what constrains them |

These two altitudes are **held at the same time**, not sequenced.

**Supporting exercises (from the GTAA AI Vision Workshop):**

- **Exec Vision Panel** — *opens the workshop.* Front-load it before other work; put leadership
  on the record; ask for ambition; carry that frame down into the rest of the day. It anchors
  the work in a transformation vision and licenses bold thinking below. Output: a shared
  strategic frame.
- **User Interviews & Floor Tour** — *grounds the work in operational reality.* Place it after
  the vision is set; go to the floor; ask how decisions actually get made; carry what you heard
  back into the room. Surfaces workarounds, builds empathy fast, and lets later concepts trace
  back to real evidence.

### M03 · Facilitate through provocation and synthesis

Guide strongly, but never dominate. Good facilitation is a **loop** that turns raw input into
shared understanding:

1. **Provoke** — set up a tension that reveals assumptions and trade-offs.
2. **Respond** — let people react; call on quiet voices and outliers.
3. **Cluster** — invite specific groups to explain their rationale.
4. **Synthesise** — translate raw input into useful, shared framing.

Three facilitation disciplines sit inside this loop:

- **Use synthesis as leadership (converge, don't concentrate).**
  - *Do:* summarise what is emerging and reflect it back so the room feels guided.
  - *Don't:* act as the smartest person in the room — answering every question concentrates
    airtime and ownership never transfers.
- **Follow curiosity, not the script (branch vs rail).**
  - *Do:* abandon the flow when a rich thread appears; let it develop; the best insight often
    lives off-script (e.g. adoption barriers).
  - *Don't:* protect the agenda at the cost of the insight — cutting threads turns the workshop
    into a checklist.
- **Surface tacit knowledge (above vs below the line).** *(strongest pattern)*
  - *Do:* probe for what's *held in heads* — judgement, exceptions, workarounds, incentives —
    not just what's *encoded in systems*. One question here often unlocks the most.
  - *Don't:* collect only the obvious, system-level facts and miss the judgement that makes
    the work actually run.

### M04 · Manage energy, safety & tone

Calibrate the room between two failure modes on each axis:

| In range | Overshoot |
|----------|-----------|
| Informal | Sloppy |
| Structured | Stiff |
| Collaborative | Performative |

Four levers:

1. **Humour** — use it lightly to lower the stakes.
2. **Openness** — name it when things feel tense or stuck.
3. **Reassurance** — you're not there to test people.
4. **Structure** — hold questions for the right moment.

### M05 · Keep coming back to value and action

A workshop is not discussion for its own sake. Orient everything toward four questions:

1. What are we actually trying to solve?
2. What does this mean for the MVP?
3. What has to be true to move forward?
4. What would genuinely create value?

> Exploratory, but never vague.

**Connect today's talk to tomorrow's decision (lands vs piles up).** Translate discovery into
direction: keep asking "what does this mean?", pull every thread toward a decision. Insight
without a connected decision just piles up — great conversation, no decision, and the workshop
ends flat.

## 4. Worked examples

Real moments that show the mindset in practice.

- **M03 · Provocation & synthesis — energy company.** Eight SVPs, eight different views. Using
  **Future Spectrums**, the facilitator put the provocation on the wall, let people land at
  opposite ends, then stepped back and let the exercise do the work — synthesising only when it
  drifted. *"I put the provocation up and let the room argue with it."*
- **M03 · Synthesis as leadership — automotive.** Nearly thirty people. Let the discussion run
  its full length, then turned it: *"I heard this from that group…"* — playing the pattern back,
  naming it out loud, and checking it ("Let's write that on the wall"). Naming the pattern *is*
  the leadership act.
- **M03 · Follow curiosity — manufacturing.** The **Impact / Effort** matrix became a live
  conversation. Printed posters flex where software can't, so bring plenty of them: same grid,
  different framing, because that's what the room needed in the moment.
- **M04 · Create safety before honesty — Coca-Cola.** The executives were guarded; it was the
  *people around them* who opened up once asked. *"Often it's the people who feel the problem
  who name it."* Safety isn't a warm-up — it's what changes the workshop.
- **M04/M05 · Respect expertise publicly — airport.** Kept a seat open for a practitioner with a
  real job to do. Whenever he was in the room the facilitator elevated him in front of everyone:
  *"I'd really love your view on this."* The rule: if you've got users or operators in the room,
  put them on a pedestal — out loud.
- **M04 · Surface tacit knowledge — automotive.** Leadership's AI product story didn't match the
  floor: people had quietly stopped using an internal tool and switched to off-the-shelf
  alternatives. It never came out by asking directly — only by listening for the unsaid.

## 5. Choosing the right exercise

Every exercise has a job. Before you choose, ask:

1. What decision does this move us toward?
2. Who's in the room?
3. What should the room feel or realise by the end?
4. If the answer is "just to discuss," pick something else.

*Example — Future Spectrums:* make sure it isn't obvious; pick topics broad enough to divide the
room; the spectrum *is* the conversation.

## 6. The exercises

Ten exercises to surface perspectives, brought across from real workshops with a how-to-run for
each.

| Code | Exercise | Purpose |
|------|----------|---------|
| **E01** | Exec Vision Panel | Set strategic altitude; put leadership on the record |
| **E02** | User Interviews & Floor Tour | Ground the work in operational reality |
| **E03** | Future Spectrums | Surface strategic tension and disagreement |
| **E04** | Current vs Future State | Map the gap between today and the target |
| **E05** | Human Moat Mapping | Locate durable human advantage vs automation |
| **E06** | Barrier / Enabler Mapping | Expose what blocks and what accelerates |
| **E07** | Jobs to Be Done | Understand why people "hire" a product/outcome |
| **E08** | Impact / Effort Matrix | Prioritise opportunities |
| **E09** | Desirability, Feasibility, Viability (DFV) | Stress-test concepts across three lenses |
| **E10** | Product Definition | Converge on what to actually build |

### E03 · Future Spectrums (how to run)

Surface strategic disagreement by placing dots on a spectrum, then let the heat map become the
conversation. Keep it low-friction ("just place a dot").

1. Pick 4–6 spectrums that reveal tension.
2. Ask one question at a time.
3. Let the disagreement show, then discuss it.
4. Close each spectrum with a shared read of where the room actually sits.

- **When to run:** early in the workshop, to open up the space.
- **Intended outcome:** visible alignment (and visible disagreement) to work from.
- **Don't use when:** the topic has an obvious answer.

Example spectrum poles used in real sessions:

- Fully AI ↔ Fully human
- Mirror the operation ↔ Run the operation
- Integrator ↔ Platform
- Human-in-the-loop ↔ Human-on-the-loop

## 8. Appendices

Supporting patterns and deep-dives — reference as needed.

- **A01 · Start with meaning before method (anchored vs floating).** Explain *why* an exercise
  matters before running it. Frame every tool as a means to a bigger question; running exercises
  cold makes participation mechanical and outputs disposable.
- **A02 · Move between altitude levels.** Zoom out (vision), zoom in (detail), zoom out again.
  Pair vision questions with operational ones (e.g. *"What shouldn't be automated?"*) and build a
  bridge between the two. Living at a single altitude disconnects the two ends.
- **A03 · Exercises surface perspectives.** Pick provocations that reveal tension; keep it
  low-friction; let the resulting heat map drive the conversation.
- **A04 · Lower the energy, design the tension (two patterns).**
  - *Lower the activation energy* — make contribution easy and interpretation hard; don't front
    complex frameworks that make people decode the method instead of the problem.
  - *Design tensions on purpose* — treat disagreement as data (AI-centric vs human-centric,
    platform vs integrator); don't drive consensus too early, which hides assumptions.
- **A05 · Create safety before honesty (open vs guarded).** Arrive as learners, not
  interrogators. Openly say *"we can't know your world better than you"* — it shifts the dynamic
  and operators stop defending. Aggressive questioning makes stakeholders guarded and answers
  politically safe.
- **A06 · Audience agility (two languages, one narrative).** Speak leadership's language
  (long-term vision, transformation horizons, adoption and change) *and* the operational
  language (tools, handoffs, release, lived reality) — then converge both into one bigger
  narrative.
- **A07 · Respecting practitioners.** *"You might have an academic understanding of the work, but
  you can't truly empathise until you understand it more deeply."* Approach practitioners as
  collaborators in discovery, not data sources: **Open** (ask open questions first) → **Probe**
  (systems, decisions, KPIs, pain points, exceptions) → **Adjust** (follow what you hear, not a
  rigid script).
- **A08 · Making complexity legible.** Don't oversimplify — give complexity form. Turn a sprawling
  problem space into navigable tensions: hard vs soft rules; current vs future state; human
  judgement vs automation; system of record vs fragmented tools; tactical pain vs long-term
  opportunity; planning horizons vs near-term reality; strategic forks vs which path to commit;
  operational limits vs what constrains the room.
- **A09 · The soft skills that make it work.**
  1. **Calm authority** — hold the room without dominating it.
  2. **Humility with expertise** — know the craft, but make space for others.
  3. **Empathy for operators** — be curious about what makes the work hard.
  4. **Audience agility** — move between execs, engineers, and partners.
  5. **Reflective listening** — reframe input so people hear themselves.
  6. **Psychological safety** — make honesty and disagreement safe.
  7. **Narrative stitching** — connect each part so the session compounds.
- **A10 · Executive summary.** *Create clarity under ambiguity.* Combine a clear narrative arc,
  lightweight but effective exercises, strong facilitation, and deep curiosity about the real
  work — moving fluidly between big-picture vision and ground-truth detail while making everyone
  feel respected and heard.
- **A11 · The biggest lesson.** Create a room where people feel safe enough to expose
  uncertainty. The workshop didn't succeed because of spectrums, interviews or canvases — it
  succeeded because people trusted the facilitator enough to be honest. In one session: leaders
  debated AI authority, ops teams admitted process failures, planners named their tooling limits,
  and execs called adoption a bigger risk than technology. *"They're genuinely trying to
  understand our world before trying to change it."*

## 9. Facilitator notes

Candid notes on the harder-to-execute moves.

- **Exec Vision Panel.** The panel isn't about limiting the thinking of people underneath — it's
  the opposite: it gives them permission and challenges them to think differently. An exec framing
  makes subordinates *more* reliant on the process (which is what you want): they stop guessing
  what leadership thinks and start working from what leadership actually said.
- **User interviews.** Consider keeping users *absent* from the exec panel to avoid leading the
  witness. Their job is to tell you what matters in the trenches, not to perform for leadership.
  Sequence matters: **vision first, then operational truth, then ideation.**
- **Synthesis as leadership.** Harder done than said. The facilitator must lead and keep momentum
  against a schedule and many fine lines. It takes comfort with dead time, comfort being the "bad
  cop," and knowing when to move on with the right amount of information — not too much, not too
  little. Put agreement stakes in the ground the moment you hear them.
- **Safety before honesty.** So many people arrive with a solution already in their head — we do
  too. The hard part is parking your own answer long enough to hear theirs, because *theirs* is
  the one that has to survive Monday morning.
